Lunar Eclipse

Rock instrumental arranged for Classical Guitar ensemble

Score available for students around the world at GuitarCurriculum.com.

Listen to the rock version and watch the watercolour painting timelapse on YouTube.

About Lunar Eclipse


In June 2020, I had one week off work with one goal: write and record a piece of instrumental music. Lunar Eclipse is the result.


Mariette Stephenson asked me to arrange Lunar Eclipse for the Kitchener-Waterloo Guitar Orchestra. It was my first Classical Guitar Ensemble composition.


I commissioned award-winning local Waterloo artist Moumita Roychowdhury to make art inspired by Lunar Eclipse. She listened and painted, and I cherish her series of incredible watercolour interpretations.


I'm thrilled that Lunar Eclipse is available at GuitarCurriculum.com. Students all over the world can discover a rock piece for their classical guitars by a Bengali-Canadian composer.

About Hemlock Wave


Luthier Steve Doreen of Lone Tree Guitars & Ukuleles in British Columbia, Canada sold me his gorgeous fanned-fret Low-G ukulele, made from Western Hemlock. It is the best sounding instrument I own.


It's a Hawaiian instrument made by a Canadian luthier with Canadian wood. I paid tribute to Hawaii with a strummed Ukulele soloist part. I paid tribute the progressive rock elements formative to my musical taste growing up in Canada, including shifting time signatures.


Hemlock Wave was composed with a classical guitar ensemble trio format in mind, and the Ukulele is fun but optional - the Guitar 1 part can be played on Guitar (with capo) or Low-G Ukulele.


Hemlock Wave was awarded 1st place the Adult 19+ Original Ensemble Composition category at the OMFA (Ontario Music Festivals Association) 2024 Virtual Festival.

Ballad of the Marsh Monster

Score available by request.

About Ballad of the Marsh Monster


I was reading a book to my children. Lyrics to an imaginary song filled the page. This is the melody I sang to my kids as I read.


The ebbs and flows represent a journey through a marsh, getting closer and closer to the monster.


You can expect to hear a phrase from The Two Grenadiers by Schumann. It's a piece from the Suzuki repertoire, and the decade or more of hearing it must have subconsciously informed the melody I sang to my kids. What better way to represent violent escalation in the search for a monster than the actual musical tone of soldiers, borrowed from Schumann.
